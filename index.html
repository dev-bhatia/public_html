Engineering Undergrad

Dev Bhatia

Class of 2021

This is my reflection on the undergrad engineering experience and some stuff I learned along the way. A little bit about me - I studied electrical engineering at UC San Diego, was part of some student orgs, worked on-campus at the Career Center, volunteered at a neuroscience research lab (Shtrahman Lab), and held internships at Intel, Tesla, and Microsoft during my undergrad. Obviously, this is all just my opinion.



"Be T Shaped" was one of the best pieces of advice I got near the end of my freshman year. The horizontal bar at the top of the "T" represents all that you know, and the vertical bar in the middle represents the depth to which you know something. I've observed that in most engineering curriculums at UCSD, students gain an in-depth understanding of their major only. This is not sufficient.

Generally there are three main fields of engineering (software, electronics, and mechanical), within which there are different majors/disciplines. I know there's also bio/chem/nano and many others that do not fit into these, but the point is don't limit yourself to just one. For example, if you major within software engineering, complement that with some electronics fundamentals ("People who are really serious about software, should make their own hardware" - Alan Kay ).

Sure, I could pair different majors and go into how they complement one another, but ultimatley what it comes down to is to have a good understanding of the digital (software, computing) and physical (electrical hardware, robotics, structures) worlds as much as you can. I cannot stress this enough.


Some books I found worthwhile - some technical, others career oriented, and others just for fun.

My Inventions
by Nikola Tesla
Sometimes we forgot what it means to invent or why even continue studying engineering - this short autobiography reinstills the spirit of innovation & excitement to engineer.

The No B.S. Guide to Linear Algebra
by Ivan Savov
A very striaghforward approach to learning one of the most fundemental topics in math. At the very least, you will learn how to "read" math. Knowing how to read math will expedite your ability to grasp concepts, definitions, and theorems in your couses.

Cracking the PM Interview
by Gayle Laakmann McDowell & Jackie Bavaro
Regardless of your intent to purse the PM role or your ability to engineer solutions, great teams indirectly seek out people who can access the impact and reach of their work both internally for the team and externally for users. This book helps you develop that mindest through real examples and clear guidance.

It's Not How Good You Are, It's How Good You Want To Be
by Paul Arden
Short, straightforward, and simple. Takes ~20 to 30 minutes to read. Worth every page.

48 Laws of Power
by Robert Greene
Just the laws and their descriptions in the Table of Contents.


4 Year Plan(s) This is like any other plan...it will change. Two quarters into my first year, I stopped following the recommended four year plan and made my own instead. The reason: I wanted more project based classes with hands-on learning rather than learning only theory. There are pros and cons to deviating from the recommended plan. The con: you don't take classes consistently with the same people your year (the consequence being you have to make more of an effort to get to know people each quarter in your classes). The pro: you can prioritize classes based on your needs (project based classes, graduating early, etc). Looking back, taking project based or application focused classes early helps build up your resume with tangible skill and experience and you become more capable of builings things sooner, rather than later.

(most) textbooks are a scam Always avoid purchasing textbooks. Two reasons: professors barely use them expect for assigning homework & you'll end up learning more from YouTube videos anyways. You can most likely (~95% chance) find a PDF of the book online. I want to be very clear - I am not encouraging this, just stating a probablity :)


UCSD College System UCSD has 7 colleges. No, they are not like the houses in Hogwarts no matter what anybody tells you. Colleges only matter for two things - where you live on-campus (if you decide to) and what your GEs will be. The latter is more important as most of campus is pretty nice. Sixth has the best and easiest GEs, Muir and Warren are tied for second, Marshall is just there (very neutral), ERC and Seventh have too many writing classes (avoid if you can).

Writing GEs Regardless of what college you end up in, everyone has to take a writing sequence as part of their GEs. Use them to boost your GPA as an engineering major. It all comes down to your TAs. These people have the most influence on your grades. In writing classes, go to their office hours occasionally to get feedback on your work (when, not if, you make their suggested changes) you will more likely get a higher score than if you didn't go
